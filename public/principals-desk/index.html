The Principal’s Desk

Leadership, discipline, and real perspective from inside education — introduced through legacy, influence, and the women who helped shape the voice behind the story.

By Latrisha Augustine-Pickett

I been knowing Trisha as long as I can remember. Her and my big sister Shun been best friends as long as I can remember too, and she always loved the kids — well me anyway.

This picture is from Tricia’s mom’s funeral. May she rest in peace.

So I’m gone give my earliest memory of literally riding with Shun and Trisha. This was back in Karst Park — way back in the 80s.

I always thought my big sister just loved having her little brother around, but years later I found out she really ain’t have a choice. My mama had me chaperoning them.

They bribed me talking about getting candy or popcorn or something from the store, and of course I said yes.

Trisha had the bikes. I still remember them being by the deep freezer in the storage area of the house. She had Shun’s bike and Celleste’s bike — that’s Trisha’s younger sister.

We getting ready to ride out. Shun did a test run first with me sitting on the handlebars telling me to hold on. I passed the initial inspection.

Then Trisha asked me if the handlebars was hurting me. I must’ve said yeah, because she went inside and got one of her mama’s couch pillows so I could sit on it.

So now we good.

I really don’t remember where we was going. It might’ve been Triple L — the hood store. It might’ve been Robert Lee’s house — Trish husband to this day.

I just know I wanted the candy.

So we riding. We get to Brown Bend Road. Mind you, I’m only about 3 or 4 years old — I think 3.

Next thing I know, I flipped off the handlebars. Mission aborted.

I’m crying. They comforting me. I think I still got the chips or candy or whatever to calm down and not tell my mama.

Truth be told… I probably still told my mama anyway.

But looking back now, I’m proud of both of them. They made a way for themselves coming from situations where the odds were not in their favor.

Fast forward years later: Trisha became an assistant principal in Dallas. Shun became a petroleum engineer, built a house from the ground up, then later pivoted into nursing and became a traveling nurse working assignments as far as Manhattan.

That’s why this matters. Because kids that grow up where we grew up need to see that circumstances do not determine your outcome.

This is where we hand it off to Trisha — or Mrs. Pickett now — to continue motivating the ones that came up like we did to never give up.
